Section I

Bezier Curves and Surfaces

Part 1: Bezier Curves with 1D de Casteljau Subdivision

Simple and straightforward implementation: At each step, \(n\) points are reduced to \(n-1\) intermediate points using the formula:

\[ \mathbf{p}_i' = (1-t)\,\mathbf{p}_i + t\,\mathbf{p}_{i+1} \]

After enough recursive steps the single remaining point is the point on the curve at parameter \(t\).

My implementation of BezierCurve::evaluateStep takes the current vector of 2D control points and returns the next level of intermediate points. It iterates over adjacent pairs of the input and lerps each pair at the member variable t.

Each press of E in the viewer calls this function once, stepping through the levels. Below I show a custom Bezier curve I created with 6 control points, along with every intermediate level of the de Casteljau evaluation at a fixed \(t\).

Part 2: Bezier Surfaces with Separable 1D de Casteljau

A Bezier surface patch of degree \((n, n)\) is defined by an \(n \times n\) grid of control points. It is evaluated at parameters \((u, v)\) by a separable application of the 1D de Casteljau algorithm:

  1. For each row \(i\) of control points \(\mathbf{p}_{i,0}, \ldots, \mathbf{p}_{i,n-1}\), fully apply de Casteljau at parameter \(u\) to obtain a single point \(\mathbf{q}_i\).
  2. Treat the \(n\) column points \(\mathbf{q}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_{n-1}\) as a new 1D Bezier curve and fully apply de Casteljau at parameter \(v\) to obtain the final surface point.
Section II

Triangle Meshes and Half-Edge Data Structure

Part 3: Area-Weighted Vertex Normals

Flat shading assigns one normal per face, producing a faceted appearance. Phong shading interpolates per-vertex normals across the face, which requires a smooth normal estimate at every vertex. I computed this by iterating over all faces incident to the vertex, weighting each face's normal by its area, summing the results, and normalizing.

The area of a triangle with edge vectors \(\mathbf{e}_1\) and \(\mathbf{e}_2\) is \(\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{e}_1 \times \mathbf{e}_2\|\). The direction of \(\mathbf{e}_1 \times \mathbf{e}_2\) is also the face normal, so the area-weighted normal for one face is simply the raw cross product \(\mathbf{e}_1 \times \mathbf{e}_2\) (the \(\frac{1}{2}\) cancels after normalization). My implementation traverses the one-ring around the vertex with the twin–next idiom: 

Vector3D Vertex::normal() const {
    Vector3D n(0, 0, 0);
    HalfedgeCIter h = halfedge();
    do {
        if (!h->face()->isBoundary()) {
            Vector3D p0 = h->vertex()->position;
            Vector3D p1 = h->next()->vertex()->position;
            Vector3D p2 = h->next()->next()->vertex()->position;
            n += cross(p1 - p0, p2 - p0);
        }
        h = h->twin()->next();
    } while (h != halfedge());
    return n.unit();
}

I skipped boundary faces with isBoundary() to avoid including the virtual boundary face in the average.

Part 4: Edge Flip

This was pretty straightforward to implement since no new mesh elements are created or destroyed; only pointers are reassigned.

I first checked whether the edge is a boundary edge and returned immediately if so. Then I named every mesh element involved (all 6 inner half-edges of the two triangles (h0–h5), their 4 outer twins, 4 vertices (a, b, c, d), 5 edges, and 2 faces) and drew the before and after configurations on paper. After the flip, e0 becomes edge \((a, d)\): face f0 holds half-edges \(a\to d\), \(d\to c\), \(c\to a\) and face f1 holds \(d\to a\), \(a\to b\), \(b\to d\). I called setNeighbors on all 6 inner half-edges and reassigned halfedge() for every vertex, edge, and face. I did not re-call setNeighbors on the outer twins since their next, vertex, edge, and face fields are unaffected by the flip.

The debugging issue I encountered was a missing face field update inside one setNeighbors call.

Part 5: Edge Split

I allocated the new vertex and set its position to the midpoint of the original edge's two endpoints. After drawing out the full diagram I wrote out every setNeighbors call and halfedge() assignment.

The two original faces are reused (f0 becomes triangle \(b,m,a\) and f1 becomes \(m,c,a\)) and two new faces are added for \(b,d,m\) and \(d,c,m\). The original edge e0 is reused as the \(b\text{-}m\) segment; a new edge em covers \(m\text{-}c\). Two new diagonal edges ea (\(m\text{-}a\)) and ed (\(m\text{-}d\)) are marked isNew = true. em is marked isNew = false because it lies along the original edge and must not be flipped during Loop subdivision. The new vertex \(m\) is also marked isNew = true.

My singular, uneventful debugging story: I initially set m->halfedge() = h0, but after reassignment h0 becomes \(b \to m\) — pointing toward \(m\), not away from it. The fix was to assign m->halfedge() = h3, which after reassignment is \(m \to b\), an outgoing half-edge from \(m\) along the original split edge.

Extra Credit:

splitEdge was extended to handle edges on the mesh boundary. A boundary edge has one real face and one virtual boundary face. Splitting it inserts a midpoint vertex \(m\) and produces two real triangles instead of four, while the boundary loop is re-routed through two new boundary half-edges on either side of \(m\).

The key differences from the interior case is that there is no opposite vertex \(d\), so only two new edges are created: em (\(m\text{-}c\), marked isNew = false since it lies on the original edge) and ea(\(m\text{-}a\), the new interior diagonal, marked isNew = true). Only one new real face is created (the \(m, c, a\) triangle). Four new half-edges are created: \(m\to a\), \(a\to m\), \(m\to c\) (real face side), and \(m\to b\) (boundary loop side). The old boundary half-edge \(c\to b\) is repurposed as \(c\to m\) and re-pointed into the boundary face, while the new \(m\to b\) half-edge is inserted next to it in the boundary loop. I walked the boundary loop to find the half-edge whose next was \(c\to b\) and re-pointed it to maintain loop continuity. The new vertex's halfedge was set to \(m\to c\) (real-face side), pointing along the original split edge direction.

Part 6: Loop Subdivision for Mesh Upsampling

Implemented algorithm:

  1. For each original vertex with degree \(n\), compute the Loop subdivision weighted average: \[ \mathbf{p}_{\text{new}} = (1 - n\,u)\,\mathbf{p} + u\,\sum_{\text{neighbors}} \mathbf{p}_j \] where \(u = \tfrac{3}{16}\) if \(n = 3\), otherwise \(u = \tfrac{3}{8n}\). I stored this in Vertex::newPosition and set isNew = false on each original vertex.
  2. For each original edge between vertices \(A\) and \(B\) with opposite vertices \(C\) and \(D\), store \(\tfrac{3}{8}(A+B) + \tfrac{1}{8}(C+D)\) in Edge::newPosition.
  3. Split every original edge. To avoid iterator invalidation and infinite loops from newly created edges, collected all original edges into a std::vector<EdgeIter>, then split each one in sequence. After each split copy the pre-computed Edge::newPosition into the new midpoint vertex's newPosition.
  4. Flip new edges connecting an old and a new vertex. An edge created by splitting that connects one isNew == false vertex and one isNew == true vertex is a diagonal that needs to be flipped to complete the 4-1 subdivision pattern.
  5. Copy pre-computed positions into all vertices. Write Vertex::newPosition into Vertex::position for every vertex. Both old vertices (whose newPosition was set in Step 1) and new midpoint vertices (whose newPosition was set in Step 3) are updated here.

Sharp corners and edges become progressively smoother with each subdivision round. The Loop weighting scheme pulls vertex positions toward the average of their neighbors, rounding off high-curvature regions. After just a couple of iterations a mesh like the cube loses all its crisp corners and begins to look like a rounded blob.

The default cube.dae has a single diagonal edge on each square face. The two corner vertices connected by that diagonal end up with higher degree than the other two corners. Because the Loop weight \(u = 3/(8n)\) decreases with degree \(n\), higher-degree vertices smooth more aggressively toward their neighbors. This unequal smoothing compounds with every subdivision round, producing a blob that clearly favors the diagonal direction of each face rather than respecting the cube's full symmetry.

To get symmetric subdivision I pre-processed the cube by splitting the diagonal edge on every face so that each square face contains an X-shaped crossing of two diagonals. This gives all eight cube corners identical vertex degree (6) and makes the connectivity symmetric under the cube's symmetry group. After this pre-processing the mesh subdivides symmetrically.

Extra Credit: Loop Subdivision on Meshes with Boundary (+2 pts)

I extended upsample to correctly handle meshes that have boundary loops. The standard Loop subdivision rules do not apply at the boundary; different formulas are required for both boundary vertices and boundary edge midpoints.

Boundary edge midpoints use a simple average of their two endpoints rather than the interior 3/8 + 1/8 weighted formula: \[ \mathbf{p}_{\text{new}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(A + B) \] This is applied whenever e->isBoundary() is true in Step 2.

Boundary vertices use a rule that blends the vertex with its two boundary neighbors — the vertices immediately adjacent to it along the boundary loop: \[ \mathbf{p}_{\text{new}} = \tfrac{1}{8}\,\mathbf{p}_{\text{left}} + \tfrac{3}{4}\,\mathbf{p} + \tfrac{1}{8}\,\mathbf{p}_{\text{right}} \] To find the two boundary neighbors, I used the fact that the halfedge of a boundary vertex points to the boundary face. One neighbor is h->twin()->vertex() and the other is h->next()->twin()->vertex(), walking in opposite directions along the boundary loop. Interior vertices adjacent to the boundary still use the standard Loop rule unchanged.

During Step 4, I also skipped flipping boundary edges (they connect to only one real face and cannot be flipped). Step 3 uses the boundary-aware splitEdge from the Part 5 extra credit to correctly handle boundary edges during subdivision.
